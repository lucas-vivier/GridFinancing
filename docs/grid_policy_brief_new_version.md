# Who Pays for Europe’s Grid?

## A financing triage for cross-border interconnection

### Executive summary

Europe needs around €150 billion of investment in cross-border interconnection by 2040. The Commission has proposed a major increase in the Connecting Europe Facility for Energy (CEF-E), from about €6 billion in 2021-2027 to about €30 billion in 2028-2034, but this would still not be enough to close the financing gap. That financing gap has been visible for years, but it is becoming more pressing as variable renewable electricity expands and the cost of delay increases.

The key policy question is no longer only how to build the grid, but how to finance it in a more effective and targeted way. This policy brief argues that Europe does not face one interconnection financing problem, but three. Some corridors may be able to attract private capital if the regulatory framework is better adapted. Some projects are blocked because countries that could in principle finance them cannot agree on cost allocation. Others are stalled because one side cannot bear the upfront cost or associated risks, even where the long-term system value is high.

Europe will likely need both additional public resources and better mobilisation of private capital, but it also needs to use existing public money more efficiently. That requires targeting support to the projects that truly need it. In practice, that means using market-based models selectively where commercial conditions are strong, applying clearer cost-allocation rules where capable countries cannot agree, and concentrating public support and risk-sharing instruments where financing constraints make socially valuable projects impossible. If this triage were applied systematically, scarce public support could be directed more consistently toward the projects that need it most.

The timing matters because the legislative window is open now. The Commission’s 2025 Grids Package has already put forward proposals on planning, permitting and cross-border cost allocation, and these proposals are now moving into the EU legislative process. That makes this the right moment to address what is still missing: an explicit financing triage linked to project characteristics.

### 1. Europe needs financing triage, not one-size-fits-all support

Projects in the interconnection pipeline do not face the same implementation bottleneck. Some could attract private capital, but the current framework still treats merchant development as an exception. Some are blocked because countries that could in principle finance the project cannot agree on cost allocation. Others are stalled because one side cannot bear the upfront cost or associated risks. These three cases call for three different instruments.

- **Track 1: market-financeable projects.** Corridors where expected congestion revenues and persistent price separation may support private investment, but where the current regulatory framework remains poorly suited to merchant-style development.
- **Track 2: cost-allocation disputes between capable countries.** Cases where the project can in principle be financed, but countries cannot agree on who should pay.
- **Track 3: credit-constrained projects.** Cases where at least one side lacks the borrowing capacity, sovereign backing or risk-bearing ability needed to proceed.

The current framework does not explicitly classify projects in this way. That is the missing piece. A smarter framework would link financing instruments to the actual bottleneck each project faces.

### 2. Where markets can finance interconnectors, EU law should get out of the way

Some interconnectors can be financed largely or fully by private capital. That should not be overstated: the number of clear cases remains limited, and the model is not suitable for every corridor. But the evidence shows that merchant-style investment can work under the right conditions.

Merchant-style investment should not be understood as a single model. At one end is the pure merchant interconnector, where investors rely primarily on market revenues and bear most price and volume risk. ElecLink, between France and the United Kingdom through the Channel Tunnel, is the clearest recent example: it was developed as a privately financed interconnector and shows that strong congestion value can support market-based recovery, with revenue of about €560 million in 2023 and about €280 million in 2024 despite market normalisation and outages. BritNed, between the Netherlands and the United Kingdom, provides an earlier example of a merchant-style link operating under an exempted model. But private capital does not have to mean exposure to fully unhedged market risk. Intermediate arrangements can make revenues more predictable and therefore improve bankability. NeuConnect, between Germany and the United Kingdom, illustrates this through a cap-and-floor regime, which keeps a market-based logic while limiting downside and upside and can therefore make debt financing easier and reduce the cost of capital.

The problem is that EU law still treats the merchant route as an exception. Article 63 of Regulation 2019/943 requires a case-by-case exemption and places a heavy evidentiary burden on the developer. That architecture is poorly suited to a system where renewable penetration, volatility and recurring price spreads may make merchant-style finance more plausible on some corridors over time.

This should remain a selective option, not a blanket liberalisation. Merchant or merchant-like models are most plausible on corridors with persistent price separation, repeated congestion and structurally different generation profiles, for example some hydro-to-thermal, wind-to-load or offshore integration corridors. Competition can help reduce the amount of public support needed, but only if there are enough credible bidders. Where competition is too weak, the result may simply be expensive support or poor project delivery. That is why a lighter route should be both selective and, where support is involved, genuinely competitive rather than automatic.

European Union law should therefore no longer treat all interconnectors as if they needed the same financing model. For corridors where market revenues are plausibly strong enough, Article 63 should offer a lighter route for merchant-style projects. Where pure merchant finance is too risky, the European Union could allow intermediate tools, such as cap-and-floor regimes, InvestEU guarantees or similar mechanisms. Once a corridor is identified as suitable for this track, the European Union, a national regulator or ACER could allocate the support instrument competitively, for example by awarding the project or the de-risking mechanism to the developer requiring the least public support.

### 3. Where partners are financially capable, the issue is cost allocation

When capital markets do not solve the financing problem, the obstacle is often not an absolute lack of money but a lack of agreement on who should pay. The existing cross-border cost allocation framework under TEN-E was meant to solve this problem, but in practice it has not done so effectively for electricity. The European Union Agency for the Cooperation of Energy Regulators (ACER) reports 65 percent fewer cross-border cost allocation decisions in 2019-2024 than in 2014-2019, with costs still remaining national in most cases. More broadly, the countries that benefit from an interconnector do not necessarily contribute in proportion to the benefits they receive: a hosting country may bear the physical and financial burden of a project while neighbouring systems capture part of the welfare gain through lower prices, greater flexibility, reduced curtailment or improved security of supply, and non-hosting countries rarely contribute financially even when they benefit.

The proposed revision of the Regulation on trans-European energy infrastructure (TEN-E), as part of the wider Grids Package, already moves in the right direction. It strengthens the logic of benefit-based allocation and allows ex post adjustments. But it still leaves too much ambiguity when negotiation fails.

The missing reform is a clearer fallback rule. When national regulators cannot agree within the legal deadline, the European Union Agency for the Cooperation of Energy Regulators should be empowered to apply a standard allocation methodology under a more explicit legal mandate. The default should remain benefit-based, but with room for clearly defined flexibility where the upfront financing burden is sharply asymmetric. That would make the framework more predictable without pretending that every dispute can be settled by ad hoc bargaining.

This only works if the underlying modelling is trusted. The cost-benefit analysis should therefore be run under a transparent and reproducible framework, with published code, assumptions, and standardised sensitivity tests. The European Network of Transmission System Operators for Electricity could continue to run the modelling, given its data and operational expertise, but under a methodology certified by the European Union Agency for the Cooperation of Energy Regulators against clear open-science requirements. That would not transfer planning to the European Union Agency for the Cooperation of Energy Regulators. It would mean that the agency validates the framework and publication standards so that Member States, regulators and outside analysts can scrutinise and, as far as possible, replicate the results.

A further step would be to use congestion income more flexibly as a compensation instrument. The purpose would be practical: to make deals easier to finance. A country facing tighter financing constraints could contribute less upfront and, in exchange, receive a smaller share of future congestion revenues, while the financially stronger side would bear more of the initial cost and recover part of it over time through a larger revenue share. This would make it easier to agree on projects where the main obstacle is the upfront burden rather than the long-term value of the interconnector. In legislative terms, this means the revision of the Regulation on trans-European energy infrastructure should define the default allocation framework and the fallback role of the European Union Agency for the Cooperation of Energy Regulators more clearly, while the electricity market rules should allow congestion-income sharing to be used more flexibly as part of cross-border cost allocation agreements.

A simple next analytical step would be to test this logic on a small number of corridors, comparing a host-country burden with a stylised benefit-based allocation and showing how much upfront pressure could be reduced through a different sharing of future congestion revenues.

### 4. Where credit-constrained projects need targeted public support

The hardest cases are projects with high strategic value but weak financing capacity on at least one side. These often involve smaller systems, islands, peripheral regions or countries facing fiscal and geopolitical constraints. In such cases, the market failure is not the lack of social value. It is the inability of the relevant entities to carry the financial risk.

The Great Sea Interconnector illustrates the problem. It is a roughly €2 billion project, backed by about €660 million of support from the Connecting Europe Facility for Energy, and linked to the broader objective of ending Cyprus’s energy isolation. Yet it has faced repeated delays, disputes over cost sharing, suspended payments to Nexans by Independent Power Transmission Operator (IPTO), and geopolitical complications linked to Turkish interference with survey work. This is exactly the type of case where a generic support framework is inadequate.

A uniform grant logic does not reflect the difference between a large investment-grade TSO and a smaller or more constrained side. Public support should therefore be targeted where financing additionality is highest. This points toward more differentiated co-financing rates under the Connecting Europe Facility for Energy, combined where necessary with risk-sharing instruments backed by the European Investment Bank.

A second gap is political and geopolitical risk. For strategically important interconnectors facing exceptional third-country interference or geopolitical disruption, the EU should explore a dedicated political-risk guarantee. The logic would be similar to Multilateral Investment Guarantee Agency (MIGA)-style cover in the World Bank Group: not a general subsidy, but a targeted guarantee against clearly defined non-commercial risks such as forced construction delays, route disruption, cancellation caused by geopolitical events, or sovereign actions that prevent the project from proceeding. In practice, this could be structured through the European Investment Bank, InvestEU or a dedicated European Union facility. The mechanism would reduce a category of risk that private investors, TSOs and even sovereigns often cannot price or absorb efficiently on their own.

### 5. A practical legislative package

The legislative sequence should be stated simply, because not all of these changes sit in the same file. The Commission presented the European Grids Package on 10 December 2025. Its core legislative pillars are the proposed TEN-E revision and a separate permitting proposal. The proposed revision of the Regulation on trans-European energy infrastructure is therefore the main live vehicle for changes to project selection, cost allocation and modelling governance. That is where the most immediate amendments should be targeted. By contrast, changes to Regulation 2019/943 would more likely need to be taken up in a subsequent electricity-market file, while differentiated financing under the Connecting Europe Facility for Energy will depend on the next Multiannual Financial Framework and Connecting Europe Facility negotiations.

In the TEN-E revision:

- create a formal financing triage for projects of common interest and projects of mutual interest;
- add a clearer fallback role for the European Union Agency for the Cooperation of Energy Regulators in cases of cross-border cost allocation deadlock;
- strengthen transparency requirements for the underlying cost-benefit analysis;
- clarify that CBCA decisions can combine lower upfront contributions on one side with more flexible compensation over time.

In Regulation 2019/943 or a later electricity-market package:

- create a lighter Article 63 pathway for selectively identified corridors where merchant-style finance is viable;
- distinguish more clearly between merchant models and hybrid de-risked models;
- allow more flexible use and sharing of congestion income within CBCA agreements, with proper legal anchoring.

In the next CEF-E and MFF cycle:

- introduce differentiated co-financing rates based on financing need;
- concentrate public support where financing additionality is highest;
- pair grants with risk-sharing instruments from the European Investment Bank or InvestEU where financing constraints, rather than project value, are the core obstacle.



### Conclusion

Europe’s interconnection debate has moved beyond whether more cross-border capacity is needed. The harder question is how to finance it in a more effective way. Europe may well need more public money, but it also needs to make better use of the money already available. The answer is not one instrument, but three. Use markets selectively where markets can work. Use clearer allocation rules where capable countries cannot agree. Use public support and risk-sharing where financing constraints would otherwise block strategically valuable projects. That financing triage is still missing from Europe’s current grid agenda.
