# Who Pays for Europe’s Grid?

## A financing triage for cross-border interconnection

### Executive summary

Europe needs around €150 billion of investment in cross-border interconnection by 2040. Even with the Commission’s proposal to raise CEF-Energy from about €6 billion in 2021-2027 to about €30 billion in 2028-2034, a substantial financing gap would still remain. That financing gap has been identified for years, but the pressure is now greater because rapid deployment of variable renewable electricity increases the value of moving faster.

The key policy question is no longer only how to build the grid, but how to pay for it intelligently. This paper argues that Europe does not face one interconnection financing problem, but three. Some corridors may be able to attract private capital if the regulatory framework is better adapted. Some projects are blocked because countries that could in principle finance them cannot agree on cost allocation. Others are stalled because one side cannot bear the upfront cost or associated risks, even where the long-term system value is high.

Europe will likely need both additional public resources and better mobilisation of private capital, but it also needs to use existing public money more efficiently. That requires targeting support to the projects that truly need it. In practice, that means using market-based models selectively where commercial conditions are strong, applying clearer cost-allocation rules where capable countries cannot agree, and concentrating public support and risk-sharing instruments where financing constraints make socially valuable projects impossible. If this triage were applied systematically, several billions of euros of scarce public support could likely be redirected toward the projects that need it most.

The timing matters because the legislative window is open now. The Commission’s 2025 Grids Package has already put forward proposals on planning, permitting and cross-border cost allocation, and these proposals are now moving into the EU legislative process. That makes this the right moment to address what is still missing: an explicit financing triage linked to project characteristics.

### 1. Europe needs financing triage, not one-size-fits-all support

Projects in the interconnection pipeline do not face the same implementation bottleneck. Some could attract private capital, but the current framework still treats merchant development as an exception. Some are blocked because countries that could in principle finance the project cannot agree on cost allocation. Others are stalled because one side cannot bear the upfront cost or associated risks. These three cases call for three different instruments.

- **Track 1: market-financeable projects.** Corridors where expected congestion revenues and persistent price separation may support private investment, but where the current regulatory framework remains poorly suited to merchant-style development.
- **Track 2: cost-allocation disputes between capable countries.** Cases where the project is financeable in principle, but countries cannot agree on who should pay.
- **Track 3: credit-constrained projects.** Cases where at least one side lacks the borrowing capacity, sovereign backing or risk-bearing ability needed to proceed.

The current framework does not explicitly classify projects in this way. That is the missing piece. A smarter framework would link financing instruments to the actual bottleneck each project faces.

### 2. Where markets can finance interconnectors, EU law should get out of the way

Some interconnectors can be financed largely or fully by private capital. That should not be overstated: the number of clear cases remains limited, and the model is not suitable for every corridor. But the evidence shows that merchant-style investment can work under the right conditions.

Merchant-style investment should not be understood as a single model. At one end is the pure merchant interconnector, where investors rely primarily on market revenues and bear most price and volume risk. ElecLink, between France and the United Kingdom through the Channel Tunnel, is the clearest recent example: it was developed as a privately financed interconnector and shows that strong congestion value can support market-based recovery, with revenue of about €560 million in 2023 and about €280 million in 2024 despite market normalisation and outages. BritNed, between the Netherlands and the United Kingdom, provides an earlier example of a merchant-style link operating under an exempted model. But private capital does not have to mean exposure to fully unhedged market risk. Intermediate arrangements can make revenues more predictable and therefore improve bankability. NeuConnect, between Germany and the United Kingdom, illustrates this through a cap-and-floor regime, which keeps a market-based logic while limiting downside and upside and can therefore make debt financing easier and reduce the cost of capital.

The problem is that EU law still treats the merchant route as an exception. Article 63 of Regulation 2019/943 requires case-by-case exemption and places a heavy evidentiary burden on the developer. That architecture is poorly suited to a system where renewable penetration, volatility and recurring price spreads may make merchant-style finance more plausible on some corridors over time.

This should remain a selective option, not a blanket liberalisation. Merchant or merchant-like models are most plausible on corridors with persistent price separation, repeated congestion and structurally different generation profiles, for example some hydro-to-thermal, wind-to-load or offshore integration corridors. The policy risk is obvious: if private developers cherry-pick only the easiest projects, public actors are left with the harder but socially necessary ones. That is precisely why a lighter route should be conditional and competitive rather than automatic.

EU law should therefore no longer treat all interconnectors as if they needed the same financing model. For corridors where market revenues are plausibly strong enough, Article 63 should offer a lighter route for merchant-style projects. And where pure merchant finance remains too risky, the EU should also allow intermediate de-risking tools, such as cap-and-floor regimes, InvestEU guarantees or similar mechanisms, to make private capital bankable without defaulting to full regulation. Once a corridor is identified as suitable for this track, the EU, a national regulator or ACER should allocate the support instrument competitively, for example by awarding the project or the de-risking mechanism to the developer requiring the least public support.

### 3. Where partners are financially capable, the issue is cost allocation

When capital markets do not solve the financing problem, the obstacle is often not an absolute lack of money but a lack of agreement on who should pay. The existing cross-border cost allocation framework under TEN-E was meant to solve this problem, but in practice it has not done so effectively for electricity. ACER reports 65 percent fewer CBCA decisions in 2019-2024 than in 2014-2019, with costs still remaining national in most cases. More broadly, the countries that benefit from an interconnector do not necessarily contribute in proportion to the benefits they receive: a hosting country may bear the physical and financial burden of a project while neighbouring systems capture part of the welfare gain through lower prices, greater flexibility, reduced curtailment or improved security of supply, and non-hosting countries rarely contribute financially even when they benefit.

The proposed TEN-E revision, as part of the wider Grids Package, already moves in the right direction. It strengthens the logic of benefit-based allocation and allows ex post adjustments. But it still leaves too much ambiguity when negotiation fails.

The missing reform is a clearer fallback rule. When national regulators cannot agree within the legal deadline, ACER should be empowered to apply a standard allocation methodology under a more explicit legal mandate. The default should remain benefit-based, but with room for clearly defined flexibility where the upfront financing burden is sharply asymmetric. That would make the framework more predictable without pretending that every dispute can be settled by ad hoc bargaining.

This only works if the underlying modelling is trusted. The cost-benefit analysis should therefore be run under a transparent and reproducible framework, with published code, assumptions, and standardised sensitivity tests. ENTSO-E could continue to run the modelling, given its data and operational expertise, but under a methodology certified by ACER against clear open-science requirements. That would not transfer planning to ACER. It would mean that ACER validates the framework and publication standards so that Member States, regulators and outside analysts can scrutinise and, as far as possible, replicate the results.

A further step would be to use congestion income more flexibly as a compensation instrument. The purpose would be practical: to make deals easier to finance. A country facing tighter financing constraints could contribute less upfront and, in exchange, receive a smaller share of future congestion revenues, while the financially stronger side would bear more of the initial cost and recover part of it over time through a larger revenue share. This would make it easier to agree on projects where the main obstacle is the upfront burden rather than the long-term value of the interconnector. In legislative terms, this means the TEN-E revision should define the default allocation framework and ACER’s fallback role more clearly, while the electricity market rules should allow congestion-income sharing to be used more flexibly as part of CBCA agreements.

A simple next analytical step would be to test this logic on a small number of corridors, comparing a host-country burden with a stylised benefit-based allocation and showing how much upfront pressure could be reduced through a different sharing of future congestion revenues.

### 4. Where credit-constrained projects need targeted public support

The hardest cases are projects with high strategic value but weak financing capacity on at least one side. These often involve smaller systems, islands, peripheral regions or countries facing fiscal and geopolitical constraints. In such cases, the market failure is not the lack of social value. It is the inability of the relevant entities to carry the financial risk.

The Great Sea Interconnector illustrates the problem. It is a roughly €2 billion project, backed by about €660 million of CEF support, and linked to the broader objective of ending Cyprus’s energy isolation. Yet it has faced repeated delays, disputes over cost sharing, suspended payments to Nexans by IPTO, and geopolitical complications linked to Turkish interference with survey work. This is exactly the type of case where a generic support framework is inadequate.

A uniform grant logic does not reflect the difference between a large investment-grade TSO and a smaller or more constrained side. Public support should therefore be targeted where financing additionality is highest. This points toward more differentiated CEF-E co-financing rates, combined where necessary with EIB-backed risk-sharing instruments.

A second gap is political and geopolitical risk. For strategically important interconnectors facing exceptional third-country interference or geopolitical disruption, the EU should explore a dedicated political-risk guarantee. The logic would be similar to MIGA-style cover in the World Bank Group: not a general subsidy, but a targeted guarantee against clearly defined non-commercial risks such as forced construction delays, route disruption, cancellation caused by geopolitical events, or sovereign actions that prevent the project from proceeding. In practice, this could be structured through the EIB, InvestEU or a dedicated EU facility. The mechanism would reduce a category of risk that private investors, TSOs and even sovereigns often cannot price or absorb efficiently on their own.

### 5. A practical legislative package

The legislative sequence should be stated simply, because not all of these changes sit in the same file. The Commission presented the European Grids Package on 10 December 2025. Its core legislative pillars are the proposed TEN-E revision and a separate permitting proposal. The TEN-E revision is therefore the main live vehicle for changes to project selection, cost allocation and modelling governance. That is where the most immediate amendments should be targeted. By contrast, changes to Regulation 2019/943 would more likely need to be taken up in a subsequent electricity-market file, while differentiated CEF-E financing will depend on the next MFF and CEF negotiations.

In the TEN-E revision:

- create a formal financing triage for PCI and PMI projects;
- add a clearer ACER fallback for CBCA deadlock;
- strengthen transparency requirements for the underlying cost-benefit analysis;
- clarify that CBCA decisions can combine lower upfront contributions on one side with more flexible compensation over time.

In Regulation 2019/943 or a later electricity-market package:

- create a lighter Article 63 pathway for selectively identified merchant-viable corridors;
- distinguish more clearly between merchant and hybrid de-risked models;
- allow more flexible use and sharing of congestion income within CBCA agreements, with proper legal anchoring.

In the next CEF-E and MFF cycle:

- introduce differentiated co-financing rates based on financing need;
- concentrate public support where financing additionality is highest;
- pair grants with EIB or InvestEU risk-sharing instruments where financing constraints, rather than project value, are the core obstacle.

With more time, the next step would be to apply this triage systematically to a larger set of corridors, build a small number of worked CBCA examples, and test whether open-source models such as PyPSA-Eur can support a more transparent and reproducible allocation framework for real projects.

### Conclusion

Europe’s interconnection debate has moved beyond whether more cross-border capacity is needed. The harder question is how to finance it intelligently. Europe may well need more public money, but it also needs to make better use of the money already available. The answer is not one instrument, but three. Use markets selectively where markets can work. Use clearer allocation rules where capable partners cannot agree. Use public support and risk-sharing where financing constraints would otherwise block strategically valuable projects. That financing triage is still missing from Europe’s current grid agenda.

## Annex: methodology and assumptions

This annex explains the method in plain language. The aim is not to reproduce every line of the model, but to show clearly what question each metric is trying to answer, what sources it relies on, and what assumptions sit underneath the results.

### 1. What this methodology is trying to do

The policy question in this paper is simple: if Europe wants more interconnection, which projects can pay for themselves, which are mainly blocked by cost-sharing disputes, and which need targeted public support because one side cannot carry the financing burden?

To answer that question, the analysis applies three practical tests to each project.

- **A market test:** could the project plausibly recover its costs from the value of cross-border trade?
- **A public-value test:** is the project beneficial for Europe even if it does not pay for itself commercially?
- **A financing-capacity test:** can the relevant TSOs and governments realistically raise the money?

That is why the paper speaks of a financing triage. It is a way of sorting projects by the kind of financing problem they face, rather than treating all interconnectors as if they needed the same policy response.

### 2. What projects are covered

The base dataset covers 100 cross-border electricity transmission projects from the TYNDP 2024 project sheets. The unit of analysis is the individual project: one row per project in the working table.

The table keeps the full cross-border pipeline, including projects under construction. For financing-gap discussions, however, the most policy-relevant part of the pipeline is normally the set of projects still under consideration, in planning, or in permitting.

In the current processed output, 76 projects can be assigned to one of the three financing tracks and 24 remain unclassified because key inputs are missing. The main missing items in the current run are credit-reference data, price inputs, or transfer-capacity values.

### 3. Main sources and what each one is used for

The analysis combines four source layers, each for a different purpose.

- **TYNDP 2024 project sheets:** these are the backbone of the exercise. They provide the project list, project status, border information, transfer-capacity values, project CAPEX, and the main welfare metrics used for the social-benefit screen.
- **TYNDP 2022 CBA workbook:** this is used as fallback and cross-check material where older welfare values are still useful.
- **Local hourly electricity-price data:** this is used to approximate the commercial value of moving power across borders. In the current workflow, the price input comes from `data/european_wholesale_electricity_price_data_hourly/all_countries.csv`, which is a country-level proxy dataset rather than a full bidding-zone ENTSO-E extraction.
- **Manual reference tables:** these provide information that is not available in a single clean public dataset, especially TSO credit ratings, TSO regulated asset bases, sovereign fiscal indicators, participant-level CAPEX-share assumptions, border mappings, and any approved project overrides.

In plain terms, TYNDP tells us what the projects are and what system value ENTSO-E assigns to them. Price data helps us ask whether markets might finance them. Credit and fiscal data helps us ask whether the public or regulated side can realistically carry them.

### 4. Why the commercial screen uses congestion rents

The first question is whether a project could plausibly attract merchant or hybrid financing. For that, the model needs a proxy for the revenue opportunity created by the interconnector.

The basic logic is straightforward. An interconnector is valuable to the market when electricity prices differ across two connected areas. If power is cheaper on one side and more expensive on the other, traders are willing to pay for access to the line. The larger and more persistent the price gap, the larger the potential trading value of the connection.

That is why the model uses **congestion rent** as the commercial screen. Congestion rent is not a perfect measure of future investor revenue, but it is the clearest first-pass indicator of whether the line creates tradable market value.

The model estimates annual congestion rent by combining three elements:

- the observed price difference between the two connected markets;
- the size of the line, measured by transfer capacity;
- an assumed utilisation factor, which recognises that a line is not fully used at full value in every hour of the year.

The formula is:

```text
Estimated annual congestion rent = price spread x capacity x utilisation
Commercial ratio = estimated annual congestion rent / annualised CAPEX
```

In the current implementation, where full hourly overlaps exist, the model uses the sum of the absolute hourly price spreads over the year. This is done for a policy reason: interconnectors create value whenever there is a price difference, regardless of which direction the profitable flow runs in that hour. Using absolute hourly spreads therefore captures the total gross trading opportunity across the year, including hours when the economic direction reverses.

The base utilisation factor is 0.60. This is a simplifying assumption. It reflects the fact that commercial use is significant but not perfect: not every hour with a price difference can be monetised fully, and outages, ramping, operational constraints, and market conditions all reduce realised revenue relative to a theoretical maximum.

The interpretation is intuitive:

- if estimated trading value is higher than annualised project cost, the project looks commercially financeable;
- if it is not, the project likely needs a regulated framework, public support, or both.

This is why congestion rent is used here. It is not because congestion revenue is the only possible income stream, but because it is the most direct and observable measure of whether the market itself is likely to pay for the asset.

### 5. Why the public-value screen uses social benefits

A project can fail the commercial test and still be a good project for Europe. That is why a second test is needed.

The question here is not whether a private investor would build the line, but whether Europe as a whole gains from the project. The model therefore uses the TYNDP measure of annual socio-economic welfare gain, usually labelled `ΔSEW`, as the core public-value indicator.

This is the right metric for a policymaker because it asks the system-level question: does the project reduce total system costs and improve overall welfare enough to justify the investment? In other words, even if the project does not generate enough direct congestion revenue to pay for itself, does it still make the European electricity system better off?

The model compares that annual welfare gain with annualised project cost:

```text
Social BCR = annual socio-economic welfare gain / annualised CAPEX
```

This is a standard policy logic. It puts benefits and costs on the same annual footing.

- if the ratio is above 1, the project appears to create more annual welfare than it costs on an annualised basis;
- if the ratio is below 1, the public case is weaker and may need closer scrutiny.

The preferred welfare input in the current implementation is the TYNDP 2024 `2030NT-EU27` scenario, with fallbacks to other available 2024 and then 2022 welfare fields when needed.

The reason for using TYNDP welfare values is practical as well as conceptual. They are the most established Europe-wide attempt to measure the broader system benefits of interconnection using a common planning framework. For a policy brief, that makes them a more appropriate basis for the public-value screen than looking only at private cash flows.

### 6. Why annual costs are used instead of total CAPEX alone

Both the commercial test and the social-benefit test compare annual value with annualised cost, rather than comparing annual value with total one-off CAPEX. That is deliberate.

Interconnectors are long-lived assets. A project with a large upfront cost can still be worthwhile if it delivers value steadily over many years. Annualising CAPEX is therefore a way of making the comparison fair: it translates the upfront investment into an approximate yearly cost that can be compared with yearly benefits.

The current implementation uses a real discount rate of 5 percent and an asset life of 25 years. This produces a capital recovery factor of about 0.07095.

```text
CRF = r / (1 - (1 + r)^(-n))
Annualised CAPEX = CAPEX x CRF
```

This is a relatively strict assumption. A longer asset life would lower annualised cost and make more projects appear commercially viable. That should be kept in mind when reading the results.

### 7. Why the financing-capacity screen uses TSO RAB and credit indicators

Even when a project looks socially valuable, it may still stall because one side cannot carry the financing burden. That is why the analysis adds a third screen.

The simplest practical test is to ask whether the project is large relative to the balance-sheet capacity of the TSO that would have to fund it. The model uses the ratio of the project CAPEX share to the TSO regulated asset base, or RAB:

```text
Credit-constraint score = project CAPEX share / TSO RAB
```

The intuition is simple. A project that is small relative to the TSO asset base is usually manageable. A project that is very large relative to that asset base is much harder to finance, even if it is economically sensible.

This screen is complemented by two additional warning lights:

- whether the TSO is below investment grade;
- whether the sovereign appears fiscally constrained, defined here as debt above 100 percent of GDP and a deficit above 3 percent of GDP.

In the current implementation, a project is treated as credit-constrained if any of those conditions is triggered on either side of the interconnector. The score threshold is 15 percent of TSO RAB.

This is not meant to be a full credit model. It is a policy-screening device to identify cases where the problem is not lack of system value, but lack of financing capacity.

### 8. How projects are assigned to tracks

The current implementation uses a simple classification rule.

- **Track 1: market-financed (merchant or hybrid)** if the commercial ratio is strictly above 1.0.
- **Track 2: regulated plus CBCA** if the commercial ratio is not above 1.0 and the project is not flagged as credit-constrained.
- **Track 3: credit-constrained, targeted EU support** if the commercial ratio is not above 1.0 and at least one side is flagged as credit-constrained.
- **Unclassified** if key inputs needed for the screen are missing.

This means the model first asks whether markets can plausibly carry the project. If not, it then asks whether the regulated parties can carry it. Only when the answer to both questions is effectively no does the project fall into the targeted-support track.

### 9. Financing-stack assumptions used after classification

Once projects are placed into tracks, the model applies a stylised financing mix in order to estimate how much public support each track might require.

- **Track 1:** 100 percent private capital.
- **Track 2:** 30 percent CEF grant, 25 percent EIB loan, 45 percent TSO balance sheet and tariff recovery.
- **Track 3:** 50 percent CEF grant for non-cohesion cases or 85 percent for cohesion cases, plus 20 percent EIB loan, with the remainder on the TSO balance sheet.

These are not claims about the exact financing structure of each project. They are scenario assumptions used to illustrate what different policy approaches would imply for the overall financing stack.

### 10. Main assumptions and limitations

The results should be read as a transparent screening exercise, not as a bankable project appraisal. Three limitations are particularly important.

First, the commercial screen uses proxy price data. The current notebook relies on local country-level hourly prices rather than full forward-looking bidding-zone modelling. That makes the congestion-rent estimate useful for ranking and triage, but not a substitute for a full revenue forecast.

Second, the participant CAPEX shares are seeded assumptions unless replaced by verified CBCA decisions or sponsor agreements. For two-country projects, the seed table generally assumes a 50/50 split. For multi-country projects, it uses equal shares as a placeholder. That can materially affect the financing-capacity diagnosis.

Third, the current credit model is still effectively bilateral. For multi-country projects, the present implementation maps participants into side `A` and side `B` and can ignore third and later participants in the side-level score. That is acceptable for a first-pass policy screen, but it is not a final treatment of complex corridors.

The practical implication is straightforward. This methodology is well suited to answering a policy-brief question: which projects look market-financeable, which mainly need clearer cost allocation, and which need targeted EU support? It is not designed to replace formal CBCA decisions, project due diligence, or full forward-looking power-system modelling.
